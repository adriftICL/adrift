Why do the oceans matter?

Ocean currents

Surface ocean currents

Unlike the still water in your bathtub, the ocean is constantly moving around in what is known as currents. These play an extremely important role in transporting heat from the equator to the Polar Regions. Without them, the tropics would be even hotter and the poles even cooler.

Surface ocean currents are typically wind generated. Wind drags on the waterâ€™s surface and causes it to move and build up in the direction that the wind is blowing.

The wind in the Southern Hemisphere is deflected to the left and therefore the surface ocean currents flow in an anti-clockwise direction. The wind in the Northern Hemisphere is deflected to the right and therefore surface ocean currents flow in a clockwise direction. The reason for this wind deflection is explained by the Coriolis effect (find out more about the coriolis effect here)

The surface currents form major spirals of water, called gyres and they occur in each major ocean. They span the entire ocean basin and the water is drawn inwards until it sinks. The Coriolis effect is not present at the equator, meaning the wind is not deflected, so there are no gyres there.

Ocean gyres

Each gyre is characterised by a strong and narrow 'western boundary' current, and a weak and broad 'eastern boundary' current. It is in these gyres that plastic floating in the ocean accumulates.

Ocean eddies

Unlike gyres, which are permanent spirals of ocean currents, eddies are temporary loops of swirling water that travel long distances before dispersing.

They form when a current flows past an obstacle and creates a reverse current which then swirls into a whirlpool.

Eddies are common in the ocean and can range in diameter from a few centimetres to hundreds of kilometres.

The water inside the eddy is different to the water outside of the eddy. For example it may have a different temperature or salinity (salt content). The temperature of the water has a direct impact on the direction of the eddy.
